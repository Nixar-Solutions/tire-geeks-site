# Customer Research

Marketing copy fails when it sounds like marketers wrote it. Customer copy succeeds when it sounds like customers wrote it. This skill mines real language from real sources.

## Source priority for customer voice

1. **Google Business Profile reviews** (highest signal — local, verified, specific)
2. **Yelp reviews** (similar quality, sometimes more detailed)
3. **Reddit threads** in relevant subreddits (r/sacramento, r/cartalk, r/whatcarshouldIbuy)
4. **Facebook community groups** (Sacramento groups, neighborhood groups, car enthusiast groups)
5. **YouTube comments** on relevant videos (lift kit installs, financing reviews)
6. **G2/Capterra-equivalent** (less applicable to local services)
7. **Competitor reviews** (what customers complain about elsewhere = your opportunity)

## What to extract

For each ICP and each neighborhood:
- **Pain points (verbatim)** — exact wording of frustrations
- **Aspirational language** — what they want their vehicle/situation to be
- **Buying triggers** — events that drove them to seek a service
- **Anxiety language** — what they fear (being turned away, hidden costs, judgment)
- **Trust signals** — what built confidence in their chosen provider
- **Anti-patterns** — language that turned them OFF (corporate, condescending, salesy)

## Per-ICP customer voice patterns

### ICP A (financing-led / credit-challenged)

Common verbatim language:
- "Need tires today, can't wait til payday"
- "Bad credit but I just need [thing]"
- "No one would approve me but"
- "Tired of being turned away"
- "Soft pull, no hard inquiry"
- "Same-day approval"

Pain points: shame at counters, multiple rejection cycles, hidden fees, surprise denials at checkout.

Trust signals: explicit financing terms upfront, soft-pull language, store-level approval not chain-level.

### ICP B (custom build / lifestyle)

Common verbatim language:
- "Looking for someone who actually knows fitment"
- "Don't want to scrub or rub"
- "Stock setup got nothing"
- "Build is finally coming together"
- "[Vehicle name] needs [specific upgrade]"
- "Tired of getting talked down to about my build"

Pain points: chains refusing custom work, fitment guesswork from inexperienced installers, condescension about their build choices.

Trust signals: technician fitment expertise, real install photos, brand inventory, honest "we won't do that because [specific reason]" callouts.

## How to use voice in copy

When writing customer-facing content:
1. Lead sections with the pain point in verbatim language
2. Resolve with the offering described in similar register
3. Avoid translating customer language into marketing language
4. If a customer says "I just need tires today" — your hero says "Need tires today?" not "Looking for fast service?"

## Verbatim phrase test

After drafting copy, ask: "Did I see/hear this exact phrasing from a real customer?" If no → rewrite that section. If you can't source the phrase to a real customer, you're guessing at voice.

## Per-neighborhood voice differentiation

Same ICP can sound different by neighborhood:
- Land Park ICP B: more articulate, brand-aware ("Looking for genuine [brand] wheels")
- Carmichael ICP B: practical, truck-focused ("Need a kit that handles 33s without rubbing")
- Elk Grove ICP A: family-focused ("Just need 4 tires before our trip to Tahoe")
- Arden-Arcade ICP A: convenience-focused ("Closest shop that takes Acima")

When writing for a specific neighborhood, mine reviews/discussions specifically tied to that neighborhood for register and detail.

## Anti-patterns

- Inventing customer language ("Many of our customers say...")
- Cherry-picking only positive quotes (negative language teaches you what to avoid)
- Generalizing across ICPs (each ICP has distinct vocabulary)
- Translating customer voice into corporate voice in editing pass
