# Competitor Alternatives

Customers compare. They're choosing between you and 3-5 alternatives, even if they don't say so. Content that ignores alternatives loses to content that addresses them implicitly.

## The implicit positioning principle

Don't name competitors. Don't trash competitors. Instead, surface the specific limitations of "the alternative" the customer is considering, then resolve those limitations with your offering.

Bad: "Unlike Discount Tire, we install lift kits."
Good: "Most chain tire shops won't touch lift kits or lowering work. We do them every day."

The customer reading this knows you mean Discount Tire. You haven't named them. You haven't sounded petty. You've made the alternative's limitation visible while presenting yourself as the resolution.

## Local tire industry competitive map (Tire Geeks specific)

### Tier 1 — Direct competitors

**Discount Tire:**
- Strengths: National brand recognition, low base prices, broad stock
- Weaknesses: Won't install customer-supplied parts, won't do lifts/lowering, requires appointments, heavy upsell pressure, no in-house financing
- Implicit positioning angle: "no appointment needed", "we install what you bring", "custom work welcome"

**Big O Tires:**
- Strengths: Franchise model with some local flexibility, broad service menu
- Weaknesses: Quality varies wildly by location, financing through 3rd-party only, slower turnaround
- Implicit positioning angle: "consistent service", "in-store financing", "same-day install"

**Les Schwab:**
- Strengths: Strong brand reputation, free services (rotations), Pacific NW dominance
- Weaknesses: Higher prices, conservative on custom work, declined to install your part if not their stock
- Implicit positioning angle: "competitive prices", "we work with your build", "no upselling"

**Walmart Auto Care:**
- Strengths: Lowest prices, ubiquitous
- Weaknesses: Installation quality issues common, long waits, won't touch anything beyond basic tires, no financing
- Implicit positioning angle: "professional installation", "fast turnaround", "complex work welcome"

### Tier 2 — Adjacent competitors

**Independent local tire shops:**
- Strengths: Locally-owned, often more flexible
- Weaknesses: Inconsistent inventory, limited financing options, hit-or-miss quality, often cash-only
- Implicit positioning angle: "stocked inventory", "Acima financing", "quality guarantee"

**Mobile tire installers:**
- Strengths: Convenience (come to you)
- Weaknesses: Can't do alignments, can't do complex work, weather-dependent
- Implicit positioning angle: "alignment included", "any complexity", "shop quality"

### Tier 3 — Status quo

What customers do today instead of choosing a tire shop at all:
- Drive on worn tires until forced action
- Buy tires online, struggle to find installer
- Defer service due to credit/cost concerns

Implicit positioning: address the procrastination directly. "Don't wait until you have a flat — Acima 0% means tires this week without the hit to your budget."

## How to use this in copy

For each page section that's about a service or differentiator:
1. Identify which competitor a customer might choose instead
2. Surface that competitor's limitation in customer language (not "Discount Tire sucks", instead "most chain shops won't do this")
3. Present your offering as the resolution
4. Move on — don't dwell

The competitor-positioning content should be ~10-15% of the page, not more. Customers don't want to read about competitors; they want to know why they should choose you.

## Anti-patterns

- Naming competitors directly (looks petty, can trigger lawsuits)
- Multiple competitor callouts in one section (looks defensive)
- Negative language about competitors ("they're terrible at...")
- Comparison tables (look bitter; better to integrate competitive points into prose)
- Making yourself look better by trashing alternatives (audiences sense it)

## The "would a real shop owner say this" test

After writing competitive positioning content, ask: "Would Tire Geeks' actual owners (Owen, Anwar) say this in a face-to-face conversation with a customer?"

A real shop owner says: "Most chain shops won't even look at custom work — sorry you got that runaround. We do that all day."

A real shop owner doesn't say: "Discount Tire's lift kit policies are restrictive and customer-unfriendly."

Match the spoken-conversation register, not the corporate-marketing register.
